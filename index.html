Week 5: 10/02-10/06 - After finishing our Computer Engineering unit, we moved on to Bioengineering. We had to make a prosthetic hand that could pick up an empty, plastic water bottle. We worked in groups of two or three, working on the hand for almost an entire period. Me and my partner originally cut out an oversized hand from cardboard and tried to start brainstorming. We had the fingers spread out, but determined it would be better to have them all line up. So, we used the first cutout as a test subject. We tried threading the string through holes in the fingers, but we couldn't get it to fold right. We taped the string to the tip of the finger and tried to figure out how to secure the rest of the tape along the finger. We knew that taping the string down would probably not work as when we pulled the string it would just break the cardboard. We decided on taping the string down, but putting a smaller piece of tape over part of the sticky part where the string would rest. So, the string had something to slide on so when we pulled the string it would bunch up and bend the finger. We cut slits in the fingers to make them bend better and taped the middle and ring finger together, as we had originally made them too thin. We made individual strings for each finger and taped them together at the bottom. We tested it out, but it was awkward to just hold the had. So we added a cardboard arm and it ended up being able to pick up the water bottle. There are pictures of our design and a video of it working below.

Spaghetti Bridge Analysis - Civil Engineering

  1. Our first, test iteration of the spaghetti bridge weighted 30.9 grams. The second, final iteration weighted 29.6 grams.
  2. Our first design was able to hold about 525.3 grams of water. Our final iteration of the spaghetti bridge held 1263 grams of water.
  3. Our first design got a score of about 17 (dividing the water held by the bridge weight). However, our final design got a score of 42.6, earning it second place of 10 groups, behind group 7 with a score of 44.4.
  4. The ends of our bridge ended up being the heaviest parts, although we did cut down on the amount of glue from our first test. This is due to the fact that we put barely any glue on the middle of the bridge, hoping to allow for more flexibility. However, the ends had most of the glue making them the heaviest because the glue contributed to most of the weight.
  5. The inspiration for our design came from researching types of bridges and what made them able to support a heavy load. We learned that crisscrossing, triangles, tight features, and some flexibility would be beneficial. So, we tried to limit glue and use triangle and crisscross supports only towards the ends of the bridge to allow for lots of flexibility in the center while still providing support. We also made our bridge with 2 layers of spaghetti that were tightly packed together, allowing for additional support.
  6. The design worked better than expected, as, during the design process there were multiple breaks in the bridge that we had to repair. One of these end breaks even resulted in the end to be curved up instead of straight. We expected it to snap in the middle, where one of the breaks was, but our repair job and the flexibility of the middle of the bridge made it survive much longer.
  7. The bridge snapped at the ends both times. I estimated the point of failure to be where the bridge broke during the design process on the ends of the bridge.
  8. A little less glue on the ends of the bridge could allow for more flexibility. This change could have potentially prolonged the snapping at the ends of the bridge. Also, if the design process had gone smoother so that there weren't breaks in the bridge it may have been, overall, stronger.
